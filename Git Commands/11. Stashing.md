When you are halfway through a feature but suddenly need to switch branches to fix an urgent bug, you can use **stashing** to temporarily save your incomplete work without making a messy commit.

```mermaid
flowchart LR
    A["Working on Feature"] -->|"git stash"| B["Clean Working Directory"]
    B -->|"Fix urgent bug"| C["Bug Fixed & Committed"]
    C -->|"git stash pop"| D["Resume Feature Work"]
```

---

## 📥 Basic Stashing Operations

- **`git stash`**  
  Takes all your unsaved modifications (both staged and unstaged) and puts them onto a temporary clipboard, giving you a clean slate to work on.

- **`git stash list`**  
  Shows a list of all your saved stashes.

- **`git stash pop`**  
  Restores your absolute most recent stashed change back onto your working files and permanently removes it from the stash list.

- **`git stash apply`**  
  Re-applies your most recent saved stash entry back onto your files but keeps its record safely in the stash list for future use.

- **`git stash drop`**  
  Permanently deletes a specified stash entry from your temporary clipboard list.

- **`git stash clear`**  
  Wipes out every single entry stored inside your stash list, deleting all stashes at once.

> [!CAUTION]
> `git stash clear` deletes ALL stashes permanently — there is no undo! Consider using `git stash drop` to remove stashes one at a time instead.

- **`git stash save "message"`**  
  Saves your current modifications to the stash while adding a custom descriptive message for quick identification later.

```diff
- git stash save "message"   # Old deprecated syntax
+ git stash push -m "message" # Modern correct syntax
```

> [!TIP]
> The modern syntax is `git stash push -m "message"` — `git stash save` still works but is considered deprecated.

---

## 🔍 Working with Specific Stashes & Inspecting Contents

- **`git stash show`**  
  Shows a brief summary of the changes held inside a stash.

- **`git stash show -p`**  
  Shows the full patch/code changes inside the stash, displaying the exact line-by-line differences.

- **`git stash apply <stash-id>`**  
  Applies a specific historical stash entry (e.g., `stash@{2}`) instead of defaulting to the latest one.

- **`git stash pop <stash-id>`**  
  Pops and removes a specific historical stash entry instead of the latest one.

---

## 🎛️ Advanced Stashing Filters

- **`git stash --staged`**  
  Creates a temporary stash containing only your currently staged changes, leaving all unstaged files untouched in your working directory.

- **`git stash --keep-index`**  
  Stashes your changes but keeps your staged files completely intact in your index.

- **`git stash -u`**  
  Stashes untracked files along with your tracked changes. *Note: Normally, standard Git stash completely ignores untracked files!*

- **`git stash -a`**  
  Stashes absolutely everything, including tracked files, untracked files, and ignored files all at once.

> [!IMPORTANT]
> By default, `git stash` only saves tracked files. Use `-u` to include untracked files, or `-a` to include everything (even ignored files).

| Flag | Tracked Changes | Untracked Files | Ignored Files |
|:---:|:---:|:---:|:---:|
| *(default)* | ✅ | ❌ | ❌ |
| `-u` | ✅ | ✅ | ❌ |
| `-a` | ✅ | ✅ | ✅ |

---

## 🌿 Creating a Branch From a Stash

- **`git stash branch <name>`**  
  Creates a completely brand-new branch starting directly from the historical commit where the stash was originally created, and then automatically applies your stashed changes onto that new branch.

  Behind the scenes, this command works exactly like running:
  1. `git checkout -b <name> <stash-original-commit>`
  2. `git stash apply <index>`
  3. `git stash drop`

> [!TIP]
> Use `git stash branch` when your stashed changes conflict with new commits on your current branch — it cleanly applies the stash in its original context.
